Accessibility & inclusion
At TIAA, we strive to make our digital experiences accessible to all audiences, including users who don’t use a mouse or touchscreen device and rely on assistive technologies and those who have different language abilities. 
We consider users who have:
Low vision
Low hearing
Cognitive disabilities
Physical disabilities
4 key areas when writing for accessibility
1 Visual
2 Audio
3 Cognition
4 Mobility
That said, we deliver content that is:  
  • Operable:  designed with intuitive navigation and accessible user interface (UI) elements.
  • Understandable: readable and scannable.
By considering everyone, you’re able to deliver robust content that functions as intended and make decisions with confidence. The following entries apply to text, visual, and audio elements across web and mobile at TIAA.
Writing for digital accessibility

Plain language

Make sure your words are human and easy to understand to avoid excluding users with lower English comprehension or those with limited understanding of financial terminology. Stick to writing at an 8th grade reading level, which allows for around 85% of the general population to understand your content. For more details on using plain language, please refer to Grammar & usage

Inclusive language

To be inclusive across all content elements, don’t use color-based terms to indicate value and avoid language that is ableist, ageist, specific to pronouns and genders, and loaded with jargon.
